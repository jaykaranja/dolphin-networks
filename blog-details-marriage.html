Bent or Broken? Unpacking the Silent Crisis in Modern Marriages

  • Monica Muiruri
  • Apr 15, 2024
  • 8 Comments

In today's world, it's becoming increasingly evident that many marriages are struggling, often teetering on the edge—quietly, almost imperceptibly—tearing under the weight of unspoken expectations, unresolved trauma, and emotional disconnection. But what's really going on beneath the surface? Are our marriages truly broken, or have they simply bent too far without the proper support?

The Unseen Struggles: Hidden Wounds and Unrealistic Expectations

Many of us enter marriage with expectations that are often unrealistic or overly idealized. We expect our partner to meet our emotional needs, fulfill our desires, and—most critically—complete us. But is this a fair burden to place on another human being? Are we expecting them to heal wounds we've never fully addressed, or meet emotional needs we haven't even begun to explore within ourselves?

A major issue lies in the unspoken traumas and emotional baggage many carry into marriage. Whether it's from childhood experiences, past relationships, or societal pressures, we bring these emotional scars with us, hoping that love will somehow act as a magical fix. But love alone isn't enough to heal deep-rooted wounds. It takes more than affection or good intentions—it takes work, self-awareness, and often, personal healing before two people can truly grow together.

When we don't heal individually, our wounds become mirrors that reflect in our relationships, leading to unhealthy patterns of communication, emotional neglect, or dependency.

Misaligned Visions, Values, and Wounds

The foundation of a successful marriage isn't just about love—it's about alignment. Do you and your partner share a common vision for the future? Are your core values in sync? And, perhaps most importantly, have both of you done the necessary emotional work to understand your own needs and traumas?

For many couples, the issue isn't a lack of love but a lack of clarity on these deeper, more foundational aspects. In some cases, couples enter marriage with mismatched values or individual wounds that never fully healed, creating cracks in the relationship from the very beginning.

One common scenario is when one partner expects the other to fulfill needs they haven't yet addressed in themselves. For example, a person who has never learned to self-soothe or set healthy boundaries may expect their partner to "fix" them, leading to frustration, resentment, and a cycle of unmet needs.

Approaching Marriage with a Past Mindset

Are we approaching marriage with a mindset from the past, attempting to apply outdated or misinformed solutions to contemporary issues? For many, the expectation of marriage as a lifelong partnership that solves all emotional and relational problems is based on historical notions of dependency and roles that no longer align with the complexities of modern life.

Societal pressures, family expectations, and generational patterns often shape our understanding of marriage in ways that no longer serve us. The traditional notion of "for better or for worse" has, for many, morphed into a mindset that tolerates suffering rather than seeking growth and healing.

We might find ourselves trying to patch up wounds from our past using outdated tools, instead of actively working together to build a future rooted in emotional maturity and mutual respect.

The Role of Parents and Societal Influence

Another layer of complexity is the preparation—or lack thereof—that we receive before entering marriage. Were we truly prepared for the realities of marriage, or were we simply prepared for the wedding day?

For many, the institution of marriage was romanticized or seen as an end goal rather than a continuous journey of growth. Parents and societal structures often prepare us for the event of a wedding but not for the long-term commitment of a partnership. We may not have been taught how to communicate effectively, set healthy boundaries, or understand the depth of emotional labor required in a relationship.

How many of us were taught about emotional wellness, mental health, or healthy communication? For most, this crucial aspect of relational education is either glossed over or entirely absent. As a result, when challenges arise, we're ill-equipped to deal with them, leaving us feeling isolated, misunderstood, or disconnected.

When Is a Marriage Broken, and When Is It Simply Bent?

The question arises: Is the marriage truly broken, or is it simply bent under the weight of unaddressed issues? We often hear about "marriages in crisis," but rarely do we hear about marriages in transition or marriages in need of healing. This distinction is crucial.

A broken marriage implies a point of no return, an irreversible fracture in the relationship. It's a state of hopelessness, where the foundation has crumbled, and the walls can no longer stand. But a bent marriage is different—it's a relationship that is strained, possibly even stretched to its limits, but still has potential to be healed. It hasn't snapped; it has simply been tested in ways that require introspection, growth, and active work to restore.

If a marriage is still bending, it can still be saved. But it requires both partners to do the inner work—healing their own wounds, adjusting their expectations, and learning healthier ways of relating. It also requires commitment to rebuilding trust, communication, and mutual respect. If the partners are both willing to grow and evolve, the relationship can bend but not break.

The Road to Healing: Communication, Awareness, and Inner Work

At the heart of any relationship, particularly in times of crisis, is the ability to communicate openly, honestly, and with emotional awareness. When we approach marriage with a sense of emotional maturity—understanding our triggers, needs, and vulnerabilities—we are better equipped to navigate the challenges that arise.

But this requires inner work first. It's not enough to ask our partner to change without also addressing our own emotional health. Healing begins with understanding ourselves—our past, our wounds, and our expectations—and only then can we move forward in a partnership with true emotional wellness.

If we continue to approach marriage with outdated mindsets, unrealistic expectations, and unresolved personal issues, we risk further damaging the relationship. But if we are willing to invest in our own growth, we can turn the bends into opportunities for deeper connection, understanding, and healing.

Conclusion: Is It Broken or Bent?

So, once more, I ask: Is your marriage broken, or simply bent? If it's still bending, there's hope. But only through healing, open communication, and a commitment to self-awareness can the relationship move from a point of crisis to one of growth and renewal.

Marriage isn't a destination—it's a journey. And like all journeys, it requires resilience, commitment, and the willingness to face the hard truths about ourselves and each other. By doing the work individually, we make the work together possible.

Let's stop asking if it's broken and start asking: Can it be saved? And if so, how do we heal? The answer lies in the willingness to do the work—both inside and with each other.
